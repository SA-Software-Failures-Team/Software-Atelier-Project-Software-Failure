Part 2: How to Exploit a 1970s Architecture

The insecure MIL-STD-1553 databus (discussed in Part 1) is a "sleeping" vulnerability. It only becomes a critical failure when an adversary finds a modern "attack vector" to gain access to it. The retrofitting of these old jets with modern, networked technology has provided exactly those entry points.


The "Trojan Horse" Pod

To keep them relevant, F-15s and F-16s are constantly fitted with new "bolt-on" pods for targeting, navigation, or electronic warfare. These pods are often built by third-party contractors and are, by necessity, plugged directly into the insecure 1553 databus to communicate with the jet.

This creates a massive vulnerability. An adversary doesn't need to hack the jet; they only need to hack the *pod*. A compromised targeting pod could, in theory:

  • Inject False Data: Tell the pilot's display he is "locked on" to a target, while secretly feeding the missile bad coordinates.
  • Impersonate Other Systems: Send false commands that *look* like they're from the navigation system, causing the jet's autopilot to drift off course.
  • Cause a Denial of Service (DoS): Simply "flood" the databus with junk data, effectively blinding the pilot and crashing his displays and sensors.

GPS Spoofing & IFF Hacking

Beyond the databus, the jet's other legacy systems are equally vulnerable. Their older GPS receivers, which are critical for navigation and "smart" bombs (like JDAMs), are highly susceptible to jamming (denying the signal) and spoofing (feeding the jet a false position).

An adversary could make a pilot think they are 20 miles off course, or cause their GPS-guided bomb to miss its target by hundreds of meters. A similar vulnerability exists in the Identification Friend or Foe (IFF) system. A sophisticated attacker could "spoof" this signal, making a hostile aircraft appear as a "friendly" on the pilot's radar, or vice-versa, with catastrophic consequences.
