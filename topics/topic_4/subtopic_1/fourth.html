Lessons Learned


The Therac-25 disaster fundamentally changed how engineers, regulators, and clinicians view the interaction between software and human safety. It demonstrated that software bugs can have lethal consequences and that assumptions about reliability must always be tested.


1. Safety Must Be Designed, Not Assumed

AECL relied on prior machine history and assumed software logic was sufficient to replace hardware interlocks. This case proved that removing physical safeguards without rigorous validation is unacceptable.


2. Verification and Testing Are Non-Negotiable

The system lacked formal unit testing, code review, or failure analysis. The lesson is clear: software controlling critical systems must undergo independent verification, simulation, and stress testing before deployment.


3. User Interface and Error Feedback Matter

The ambiguous “Malfunction” messages misled operators into continuing unsafe treatments. Human-computer interaction design must prioritize clarity, context, and safety feedback.


4. Organizational Transparency

AECL’s reluctance to share incident data delayed corrective action. The case reinforced the importance of open incident reporting and regulatory collaboration across institutions.


5. Ethics and Accountability in Engineering

Therac-25 is now a cornerstone of engineering ethics education, illustrating that developers and organizations bear a moral responsibility to anticipate failure modes and protect human life.
