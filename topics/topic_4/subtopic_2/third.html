Consequences & Extent of Patient Harm


Between August 2000 and March 2001, a total of 28 patients with prostate and cervical cancer were overexposed during radiotherapy sessions at the National Oncology Institute in Panama City. The overdoses ranged from 20% to more than 100% above prescribed levels, leading to severe radiation injuries and, ultimately, multiple fatalities.



Within months, many patients developed symptoms of radiation toxicity, including tissue necrosis, intestinal damage, and severe pelvic inflammation. Despite efforts to manage these effects, 8 patients died directly from radiation-induced injuries, while others experienced permanent complications that severely reduced their quality of life.

Systemic Consequences and Global Impact

The event also had deep systemic consequences. The Panamanian Ministry of Health temporarily suspended the hospital’s radiotherapy program, and international agencies such as the IAEA and the Pan American Health Organization (PAHO) launched a full investigation. The case revealed gaps in software validation, regulatory oversight, and staff training that extended beyond Panama, influencing global safety standards in radiotherapy.



The accident’s impact was not limited to the affected patients — it disrupted public trust in radiotherapy, led to new safety audits across Latin America, and triggered an international reevaluation of treatment planning software safety certification. The tragedy became a defining moment in modern radiological protection history.
