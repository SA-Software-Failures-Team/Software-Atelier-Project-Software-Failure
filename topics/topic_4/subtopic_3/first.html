Context

The WannaCry attack was a widespread ransomware cyberattack that occurred in May 2017. Ransomware is a type of malware that encrypts a victim's files, making them inaccessible, and demands payment to restore access. If payment is not made before the deadline, the victim risks permanent data loss.

WannaCry spread rapidly across the world, infecting hundreds of thousands of computers in over 150 countries. Many cybersecurity agencies and governments have attributed the creation and deployment of WannaCry to state-sponsored hackers linked to North Korea, who were allegedly motivated by financial gain and possibly by political goals.

North Korea has been linked to several criminal activities like counterfeiting money and pharmaceuticals as they needed ways to raise money after being heavily sanctioned. They also started launching multiple cyber attacks around the globe as another criminal way to get money. At one point, a majority of cyber attacks in South Korea were believed to come from North Korea, a country with less than 10,000 IP addresses.

The malware demanded payment in Bitcoin to a specific non-traceable wallet that would supposedly unlock infected systems once payment was received. The usage of cryptocurrency made tracing the attackers more difficult.

Cause

This attack was made possible due to a serious security vulnerability present in Microsoft Windows known as EternalBlue, which had originally been discovered by the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) months prior to the actual attack.

Instead of reporting the flaw to Microsoft immediately and risking the information getting leaked and vulnerability exploited, the NSA allegedly kept it as a powerful cyber weapon for intelligence operations.

The hacking group Shadow Brokers later leaked the vulnerability online in various illegal hacker forums, allowing criminal groups to develop ransomware using it. The difficulty was that even though many hackers now had access to this dangerous cyber weapon, they still hadn't figured out how to use it correctly. The North Korean hackers were the first to do so, having full access and understanding of this leaked information.

Once a machine was infected, WannaCry would automatically spread across networks without user action, keeping a low profile but without doing catastrophic damage immediately. Instead, it gave attackers the option to put more malicious content into the system, while also encrypting files and displaying a ransom note that threatened permanent data loss if payment was not made.

Impact

The UK's National Health Service (NHS) was among the most heavily affected organizations because many of its computers were still running outdated Windows systems lacking important security patches. In fact, a patch was released a week prior to the attack that actually fixed the exploited vulnerability. The issue was that many people were not updating their systems due to laziness, habit, or convenience.

As a result, over 80 NHS trusts were disrupted, and hospitals lost access to critical digital systems, including patient records, appointment schedules, and diagnostic equipment. Ambulances had to be diverted, surgeries were delayed or cancelled, and medical staff were forced to rely on paper-based procedures.

The temporary solution was to keep doing what they did in the past - operating the healthcare system in an "old fashioned way" without computers. While no patient deaths were directly attributed to the attack, it significantly reduced the NHS's ability to deliver urgent care.

Overall, the WannaCry attack became a wake-up call in the field of informatics, healthcare technology, and especially cybersecurity. It showed how dependent essential public services have become on digital infrastructure and how a single vulnerability can lead to massive real-world consequences that may also cause death in the worst case.

The incident pushed governments and healthcare institutions worldwide to prioritize regular system updates, network segmentation, data backups, and stronger cybersecurity policies. WannaCry also highlighted the growing risks of cyberwarfare tools falling into the hands of criminals, reinforcing the need for global cooperation in protecting critical infrastructure from future cyberattacks.
